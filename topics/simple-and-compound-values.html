Topic 1: Context and Motivation

Business transactions like deposits, withdrawals, loans, and credit purchases involve interest. Understanding simple vs compound interest helps in making wise financial decisions.

00:00 Time

What is Interest?

Interest Earned (Savings)

When you deposit money in a bank, the bank pays you interest - extra money for letting them use your funds.

Example:

If you deposit ₱10,000 at 2.5% annual interest, after 1 year you'll have:

₱10,000 + ₱250 = ₱10,250

Interest Paid (Loans)

When you borrow money, you pay interest - extra money for using someone else's funds.

Example:

If you borrow ₱10,000 at 5% annual interest, after 1 year you'll owe:

₱10,000 + ₱500 = ₱10,500

Dialogue: Janice and Jamaica's Conversation

J

Jamaica:

"Janice, why are you saving money? Shouldn't we spend it on fun things?"

J

"I'm thinking about opening a Kid Savers bank account with 2.5% annual interest. This way, my money can grow and help me pay for college in the future!"

Key Questions to Consider:

  • Why save? Saving helps you achieve future goals like education, emergencies, or major purchases.
  • What are the advantages of bank interest? Your money grows over time without you doing anything extra!
  • How much will accumulate after multiple years? This depends on whether the bank uses simple or compound interest.

Real-World Applications

Savings Accounts

Banks pay interest on your deposits, helping your money grow over time.

Loans & Mortgages

When you borrow money, you pay interest to the lender for using their funds.

Investments

Understanding interest helps you make better investment decisions.

Topic 2: Simple Interest (Is = Prt)

Compute interest, rate, time, and maturity value: Is = Prt, F = P(1+rt) = P + Is.

00:00 Time

Understanding Simple Interest

What is Simple Interest?

Simple interest is calculated only on the principal amount (the original money deposited or borrowed). It does not compound - meaning interest doesn't earn interest.

Key Characteristics:

  • Interest is calculated once per period
  • Same interest amount each period
  • Linear growth over time
  • Simple to calculate and understand

The Simple Interest Formula

Is = P × r × t

P

Principal

r

Rate (decimal)

t

Time (years)

Example Calculation:

If P = ₱10,000, r = 5% (0.05), t = 3 years:

Is = ₱10,000 × 0.05 × 3 = ₱1,500

Each year earns: ₱10,000 × 0.05 = ₱500

Maturity Value (Future Value)

The maturity value (F) is the total amount you'll have after interest is added:

F = P + Is

or

F = P(1 + rt)

Using the example above:

F = ₱10,000 + ₱1,500 = ₱11,500

or F = ₱10,000(1 + 0.05×3) = ₱11,500

Topic 4: Compound Interest & Time Value

Compute maturity value F = P(1+r/n)^(nt), compound interest Ic = F − P, and present value P = F/(1+r/n)^(nt).

00:00 Time

Understanding Compound Interest

What is Compound Interest?

Compound interest is interest calculated on both the principal and previously earned interest. This means interest earns interest, creating exponential growth over time!

Key Characteristics:

  • Interest compounds (earns interest on interest)
  • Exponential growth over time
  • More frequent compounding = higher returns
  • Better for long-term investments

The Compound Interest Formula

F = P(1 + r/n)^(nt)

P

Principal

r

Rate (decimal)

n

Compounding frequency

t

Time (years)

Example Calculation:

If P = ₱10,000, r = 5% (0.05), n = 4 (quarterly), t = 2 years:

Step 1: r/n = 0.05/4 = 0.0125

Step 2: nt = 4 × 2 = 8

Step 3: (1 + 0.0125)^8 = 1.1045

F = ₱10,000 × 1.1045 = ₱11,045

Compounding Frequency

Annually

n = 1

Semi-annually

n = 2

Quarterly

n = 4

Monthly

n = 12

Daily

n = 365

💡 Tip: More frequent compounding results in higher final amounts!

Present Value (PV)

Present Value is the current worth of money to be received in the future. It's the inverse of compound interest.

PV = F / (1 + r/n)^(nt)

This tells you how much you need to invest today to get a certain amount in the future.
