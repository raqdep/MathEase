Understanding Interest: A Complete Guide

What is Interest?

Interest is the cost of borrowing money or the return on investment when you lend money. It's expressed as a percentage of the principal amount and is calculated over a specific time period.

Simple Definition:

Interest = Money paid for using someone else's money

Key Financial Terms
Principal (P)

The initial amount of money borrowed, invested, or deposited.

Example: ₱10,000 loan

Rate (r)

The percentage charged or earned per time period (usually per year).

Example: 5% per year

Time (t)

The duration for which money is borrowed or invested (in years, months, or days).

Example: 3 years

Maturity Value (F)

The total amount to be paid back or received at the end of the time period (Principal + Interest).

Example: ₱11,500

Simple Interest vs Compound Interest
Simple Interest

Interest is calculated only on the original principal amount throughout the entire time period.

Formula:

Is = P × r × t

Maturity Value:

F = P(1 + rt)

Characteristics:

  • Interest grows linearly
  • Same interest amount each period
  • Common in short-term loans
  • Easier to calculate
Compound Interest

Interest is calculated on the principal plus previously earned interest, meaning interest earns interest!

Formula:

F = P(1 + r/n)^(nt)

Where:

n = compounding frequency

Characteristics:

  • Interest grows exponentially
  • Interest amount increases each period
  • Common in savings accounts & investments
  • More profitable over long periods

Key Difference: Simple interest grows at a constant rate, while compound interest grows faster because you earn interest on your interest! This is why compound interest is often called "the eighth wonder of the world."

Real-World Examples

Example 1: Simple Interest Loan

Scenario: Maria borrows ₱20,000 from a bank at 5% simple interest per year for 3 years.

Principal (P)

₱20,000

Rate (r)

5% = 0.05

Time (t)

3 years

Interest (Is)

₱3,000

Calculation:

Is = P × r × t

Is = ₱20,000 × 0.05 × 3

Is = ₱3,000

Maturity Value: F = P + Is = ₱20,000 + ₱3,000 = ₱23,000

Maria will pay back ₱23,000 after 3 years. The interest is the same each year: ₱1,000/year.

Example 2: Compound Interest Savings

Scenario: Juan deposits ₱10,000 in a savings account at 6% compound interest per year, compounded annually, for 3 years.

Principal (P)

₱10,000

Rate (r)

6% = 0.06

Time (t)

3 years

Frequency (n)

1 (annually)

Final Amount (F)

₱11,910.16

Calculation:

F = P(1 + r/n)^(nt)

F = ₱10,000(1 + 0.06/1)^(1×3)

F = ₱10,000(1.06)³

F = ₱10,000 × 1.191016

Final Amount: ₱11,910.16

Interest Earned: Ic = F - P = ₱11,910.16 - ₱10,000 = ₱1,910.16

Year-by-Year Growth:

  • Year 1: ₱10,000 × 1.06 = ₱10,600 (Interest: ₱600)
  • Year 2: ₱10,600 × 1.06 = ₱11,236 (Interest: ₱636)
  • Year 3: ₱11,236 × 1.06 = ₱11,910.16 (Interest: ₱674.16)

Notice how the interest increases each year! This is the power of compound interest.

Example 3: Simple vs Compound Interest Comparison

Scenario: Compare ₱50,000 invested at 8% for 5 years using simple interest vs compound interest (compounded annually).

Simple Interest

Is = ₱50,000 × 0.08 × 5

Is = ₱20,000

F = ₱50,000 + ₱20,000

F = ₱70,000

Compound Interest

F = ₱50,000(1.08)⁵

F = ₱50,000 × 1.469328

F = ₱73,466.40

Ic = ₱73,466.40 - ₱50,000

Ic = ₱23,466.40

Difference: Compound interest earns ₱3,466.40 more than simple interest! This difference becomes even larger over longer time periods.

Key Formulas & Reference

Simple Interest Formulas

Simple Interest

Is = P × r × t

Maturity Value

F = P(1 + rt)

Alternative Form

F = P + Is

Where:

  • P = Principal amount
  • r = Interest rate (as decimal)
  • t = Time period (in years)
  • Is = Simple interest
  • F = Maturity value

Compound Interest Formulas

Maturity Value

F = P(1 + r/n)^(nt)

Compound Interest

Ic = F - P

Present Value

PV = F / (1 + r/n)^(nt)

Where:

  • P = Principal amount
  • r = Interest rate (as decimal)
  • n = Compounding frequency per year
  • t = Time period (in years)
  • Ic = Compound interest
  • F = Future/Maturity value

Common Compounding Frequencies

Annually

n = 1

Once per year

Semi-annually

n = 2

Twice per year

Quarterly

n = 4

4 times per year

Monthly

n = 12

12 times per year

Daily

n = 365

365 times per year

Important Notes

  • Always convert percentage rates to decimals (e.g., 5% = 0.05) before calculating
  • Time (t) must be in the same unit as the rate period (usually years)
  • For compound interest, more frequent compounding (higher n) results in higher returns
  • Simple interest is better for short-term loans, compound interest is better for long-term investments
  • Always read loan/investment terms carefully to identify which type of interest is being used

Study Tips

✓ Practice Steps:

  1. Identify all given values (P, r, t, n)
  2. Determine if it's simple or compound interest
  3. Choose the correct formula
  4. Substitute values carefully
  5. Calculate step by step
  6. Check your answer makes sense

✓ Common Mistakes to Avoid:

  • Forgetting to convert % to decimal
  • Using wrong time units
  • Confusing simple and compound formulas
  • Not accounting for compounding frequency
  • Calculation errors in exponents
