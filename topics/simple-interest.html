Topic 1: Introduction to Simple Interest

Understand the concept, where it is used, and the core formula structure.

00:00 Time

  • Understand the formula I = P × R × T

Understanding Simple Interest

Core Concept

Simple interest is a method of calculating interest where the interest is computed only on the principal amount (the original sum of money borrowed or invested). Unlike compound interest, simple interest does not accumulate interest on previously earned interest, making it straightforward and predictable.

📐 The Fundamental Formula:

I = P × R × T

Interest = Principal × Rate × Time

I = Interest earned/paid
P = Principal (initial amount)
R = Rate per year (as decimal)
T = Time in years

⚠️ Important: Always convert the percentage rate to decimal form before calculation (e.g., 5% = 0.05). Also, ensure time is in years (convert months to years by dividing by 12).

Real-World Applications

Short-term Loans:

Personal loans, payday loans, and short-term credit facilities typically use simple interest for easier calculation.

Basic Savings:

Simple savings accounts and some certificates of deposit use simple interest, especially for shorter terms.

Discounted Bills:

Early payment discounts on invoices and bills often use simple interest calculations.

Promissory Notes:

Simple interest notes and bills of exchange commonly use this method.

Why Simple Interest?

✅ Advantages:

  • Easy to calculate and understand
  • Predictable and linear growth
  • Transparent - you know exactly how much interest you'll pay
  • Commonly used for short-term financial transactions

💡 Key Insight: Simple interest grows linearly - it increases at a constant rate over time. This makes it much easier to calculate and understand compared to compound interest, which grows exponentially!

📊 Visual Comparison: If you invest ₱1,000 at 10% simple interest for 3 years, you'll earn ₱300 total (₱100 per year). With compound interest, you'd earn more because interest earns interest!

Detailed Worked Examples

1

Example 1: Basic Simple Interest Calculation

Problem:

Calculate the simple interest on ₱10,000 at 5% per year for 3 years. Also find the total amount.

Step-by-Step Solution:

Step 1: Identify the given values:
P = ₱10,000 (Principal)
R = 5% per year (Rate)
T = 3 years (Time)
Step 2: Convert the rate from percentage to decimal:
R = 5% = 5 ÷ 100 = 0.05
Step 3: Apply the simple interest formula:
I = P × R × T
I = 10,000 × 0.05 × 3
Step 4: Calculate the interest:
I = 10,000 × 0.05 × 3 = ₱1,500
Step 5: Find the total amount:
A = P + I
A = 10,000 + 1,500 = ₱11,500

Answer: The simple interest is ₱1,500 and the total amount is ₱11,500.

2

Example 2: Simple Interest with Months

Problem:

Maria borrowed ₱5,000 at 6% per year. If she pays it back after 8 months, how much interest will she pay?

Step-by-Step Solution:

Step 1: Given values:
P = ₱5,000
R = 6% = 0.06
T = 8 months
Step 2: Convert months to years:
T = 8 months = 8 ÷ 12 = 0.667 years (or 2/3 years)
Step 3: Calculate interest:
I = P × R × T
I = 5,000 × 0.06 × (8/12)
I = 5,000 × 0.06 × 0.667
I = ₱200

Answer: Maria will pay ₱200 in interest.

💡 Note: Always convert time to years when using the formula!

Topic 2: Using I = P × R × T

Mastering the Calculation Process

The Complete Step-by-Step Process

1
Convert Rate

Convert percentage rate to decimal form by dividing by 100.

R% → r = R ÷ 100

Example: 7% → 0.07

2
Calculate Interest

Multiply principal, rate (decimal), and time together.

I = P × r × T

Example: I = 15,000 × 0.07 × 1.5

3
Find Total Amount

Add the principal and interest together.

A = P + I

Example: A = 15,000 + 1,575

Detailed Worked Example

Problem: Calculate the interest and total amount if P = ₱15,000, R = 7%, T = 1.5 years

Step 1: Convert Rate to Decimal

R = 7%

r = 7 ÷ 100 = 0.07

Step 2: Apply the Formula

I = P × r × T

I = 15,000 × 0.07 × 1.5

Step 3: Calculate Interest

I = 15,000 × 0.105 = ₱1,575

💡 Note: 0.07 × 1.5 = 0.105

Step 4: Find Total Amount

A = P + I

A = 15,000 + 1,575 = ₱16,575

Final Answer: Interest = ₱1,575, Total Amount = ₱16,575

Time Conversion Guide

Converting Months to Years:

• 3 months = 3/12 = 0.25 years
• 6 months = 6/12 = 0.5 years
• 9 months = 9/12 = 0.75 years
• 12 months = 12/12 = 1 year

Converting Days to Years:

• 90 days = 90/360 = 0.25 years
• 180 days = 180/360 = 0.5 years
• 360 days = 360/360 = 1 year
💡 Note: Use 360 days for business calculations

Practice Examples with Detailed Solutions

1

Example: Time Given in Months

Problem:

Calculate simple interest on ₱8,000 at 5% per year for 9 months. Find both the interest and total amount.

Complete Solution:

Given: P = ₱8,000
R = 5% per year
T = 9 months
Step 1: Convert rate to decimal:
R = 5% = 5 ÷ 100 = 0.05
Step 2: Convert months to years:
T = 9 months = 9 ÷ 12 = 0.75 years
Step 3: Calculate interest:
I = P × R × T
I = 8,000 × 0.05 × 0.75
I = 8,000 × 0.0375
I = ₱300
Step 4: Find total amount:
A = P + I
A = 8,000 + 300 = ₱8,300

Answer: Interest = ₱300, Total Amount = ₱8,300

2

Example: Fractional Time Period

Problem:

Juan deposits ₱12,000 in a savings account that pays 4.5% simple interest per year. How much will he have after 2 years and 3 months?

Complete Solution:

Given: P = ₱12,000
R = 4.5% per year
T = 2 years and 3 months
Step 1: Convert rate to decimal:
R = 4.5% = 4.5 ÷ 100 = 0.045
Step 2: Convert time to years:
T = 2 years + 3 months
T = 2 + (3 ÷ 12) = 2 + 0.25 = 2.25 years
Step 3: Calculate interest:
I = P × R × T
I = 12,000 × 0.045 × 2.25
I = 12,000 × 0.10125
I = ₱1,215
Step 4: Find total amount:
A = P + I
A = 12,000 + 1,215 = ₱13,215

Answer: After 2 years and 3 months, Juan will have ₱13,215

Common Pitfalls

  • Time conversion: For months, convert T to years (e.g., 6 months = 0.5 years, 9 months = 0.75 years)
  • Rate conversion: Always convert R% to decimal (divide by 100)
  • Unit consistency: Ensure all units match (years, decimal rate)

Solving for Unknown Variables

Understanding Formula Rearrangement

Sometimes in real-world problems, you know the interest but need to find the principal, rate, or time. By rearranging the simple interest formula I = P × R × T, you can solve for any variable when the other three are known.

💡 Key Concept: To solve for any variable, isolate it on one side of the equation by using inverse operations. If a variable is multiplied, divide both sides. If it's divided, multiply both sides.

P

Solve for Principal

When I, R, and T are known:

P = I / (R × T)

Principal = Interest ÷ (Rate × Time)

How it works:

Start with I = P × R × T

Divide both sides by (R × T)

Result: P = I / (R × T)

💡 Divide interest by the product of rate and time

R

Solve for Rate

When I, P, and T are known:

R = I / (P × T)

Rate = Interest ÷ (Principal × Time)

How it works:

Start with I = P × R × T

Divide both sides by (P × T)

Result: R = I / (P × T)

💡 Remember to convert decimal to percentage (multiply by 100)

T

Solve for Time

When I, P, and R are known:

T = I / (P × R)

Time = Interest ÷ (Principal × Rate)

How it works:

Start with I = P × R × T

Divide both sides by (P × R)

Result: T = I / (P × R)

💡 Result is in years; convert to months if needed

Visual Formula Derivation

Original Formula:

I = P × R × T

To solve for P:

I = P × (R × T)

I ÷ (R × T) = P

P = I / (R × T)

To solve for R:

I = (P × T) × R

I ÷ (P × T) = R

R = I / (P × T)

To solve for T:

I = (P × R) × T

I ÷ (P × R) = T

T = I / (P × R)

Comprehensive Worked Examples

1

Example 1: Finding Principal (P)

Problem:

A loan earned ₱2,400 in simple interest at 12% per year for 2 years. What was the principal amount?

Step-by-Step Solution:

Given: I = ₱2,400
R = 12% per year
T = 2 years
Find: P = ?
Step 1: Convert rate to decimal:
R = 12% = 12 ÷ 100 = 0.12
Step 2: Use the rearranged formula:
P = I / (R × T)
Step 3: Substitute values:
P = 2,400 / (0.12 × 2)
P = 2,400 / 0.24
Step 4: Calculate:
P = ₱10,000

Answer: The principal amount was ₱10,000

✅ Verification: I = 10,000 × 0.12 × 2 = ₱2,400 ✓

2

Example 2: Finding Rate (R)

Problem:

Ana invested ₱10,000 and earned ₱1,000 in simple interest after 1 year. What was the annual interest rate?

Step-by-Step Solution:

Given: P = ₱10,000
I = ₱1,000
T = 1 year
Find: R = ?
Step 1: Use the rearranged formula:
R = I / (P × T)
Step 2: Substitute values:
R = 1,000 / (10,000 × 1)
R = 1,000 / 10,000
Step 3: Calculate decimal rate:
R = 0.10
Step 4: Convert to percentage:
R = 0.10 × 100 = 10%

Answer: The annual interest rate was 10%

✅ Verification: I = 10,000 × 0.10 × 1 = ₱1,000 ✓

3

Example 3: Finding Time (T)

Problem:

How long will it take for ₱5,000 to earn ₱750 in simple interest at 6% per year?

Step-by-Step Solution:

Given: P = ₱5,000
I = ₱750
R = 6% per year
Find: T = ?
Step 1: Convert rate to decimal:
R = 6% = 6 ÷ 100 = 0.06
Step 2: Use the rearranged formula:
T = I / (P × R)
Step 3: Substitute values:
T = 750 / (5,000 × 0.06)
T = 750 / 300
Step 4: Calculate:
T = 2.5 years
Step 5: Convert to months (optional):
2.5 years = 2 years and 6 months

Answer: It will take 2.5 years (or 2 years and 6 months)

✅ Verification: I = 5,000 × 0.06 × 2.5 = ₱750 ✓

Real-World Applications of Simple Interest

Where Simple Interest is Used

Simple interest is widely used in various financial transactions. Understanding these applications helps you make informed decisions about loans, investments, and financial planning. Let's explore the most common real-world scenarios.

Personal Loans

Short-term personal loans, payday loans, and credit facilities often use simple interest for transparency and ease of calculation.

Savings Accounts

Basic savings accounts and some certificates of deposit use simple interest, especially for shorter terms.

Invoice Discounting

Early payment discounts on invoices and bills use simple interest calculations to determine the discount amount.

Promissory Notes

Simple interest notes and bills of exchange commonly use this method for interest calculation.

Bank Discount (Simple Discount)

Bank discount is a special application of simple interest where interest is charged in advance when a promissory note or bill is discounted (sold) before its maturity date. This is common in business transactions where companies need immediate cash.

📐 Bank Discount Formulas:

D = M × d × t

Discount = Maturity Value × Discount Rate × Time

Proceeds = M − D

Proceeds = Maturity Value − Discount

D = Discount (interest charged)
M = Maturity value (face value)
d = Discount rate (as decimal)
t = Time until maturity (in years)

💡 Key Difference: In bank discount, the interest (discount) is deducted upfront from the maturity value, so you receive less than the face value. The proceeds are what you actually receive after the discount is deducted.

Loan Applications

Simple interest loans are common for short-term borrowing. Here's how they work:

Example Scenario:

You borrow ₱20,000 at 8% simple interest for 6 months.

P = ₱20,000, R = 8% = 0.08, T = 6/12 = 0.5 years

I = 20,000 × 0.08 × 0.5 = ₱800

Total to repay: ₱20,800

Investment Applications

Simple interest is also used in certain investment products:

Fixed Deposits

Some fixed deposit accounts use simple interest, especially for shorter terms (less than 1 year).

Treasury Bills

Government treasury bills often use bank discount, which is based on simple interest principles.

Real-World Problem Solving

1

Example 1: Bank Discount

Problem:

A company has a ₱20,000 promissory note that matures in 90 days. They need cash immediately, so they discount it at a bank charging 8% per year. Find the discount and the proceeds they will receive.

Step-by-Step Solution:

Given: M = ₱20,000 (maturity value)
d = 8% per year (discount rate)
t = 90 days (time until maturity)
Step 1: Convert discount rate to decimal:
d = 8% = 8 ÷ 100 = 0.08
Step 2: Convert days to years (using 360-day year):
t = 90 days = 90 ÷ 360 = 0.25 years
Step 3: Calculate the discount:
D = M × d × t
D = 20,000 × 0.08 × 0.25
D = 20,000 × 0.02
D = ₱400
Step 4: Calculate the proceeds:
Proceeds = M − D
Proceeds = 20,000 − 400
Proceeds = ₱19,600

Answer: The discount is ₱400 and the company will receive proceeds of ₱19,600

💡 The company receives less than the face value because interest is deducted in advance!

2

Example 2: Personal Loan

Problem:

Carlos borrows ₱15,000 from a friend who charges 6% simple interest per year. If Carlos repays the loan after 8 months, how much interest will he pay and what is the total amount due?

Step-by-Step Solution:

Given: P = ₱15,000
R = 6% per year
T = 8 months
Step 1: Convert rate to decimal:
R = 6% = 0.06
Step 2: Convert time to years:
T = 8 ÷ 12 = 0.667 years (or 2/3 years)
Step 3: Calculate interest:
I = P × R × T
I = 15,000 × 0.06 × (8/12)
I = 15,000 × 0.06 × 0.667
I = ₱600
Step 4: Find total amount due:
A = P + I
A = 15,000 + 600 = ₱15,600

Answer: Carlos will pay ₱600 in interest, and the total amount due is ₱15,600

3

Example 3: Savings Investment

Problem:

Maria deposits ₱25,000 in a savings account that pays 3.5% simple interest per year. How much will she have after 2 years?

Step-by-Step Solution:

Given: P = ₱25,000
R = 3.5% per year
T = 2 years
Step 1: Convert rate to decimal:
R = 3.5% = 3.5 ÷ 100 = 0.035
Step 2: Calculate interest:
I = P × R × T
I = 25,000 × 0.035 × 2
I = ₱1,750
Step 3: Find total amount:
A = P + I
A = 25,000 + 1,750 = ₱26,750

Answer: After 2 years, Maria will have ₱26,750 in her account

💡 This includes her original ₱25,000 plus ₱1,750 in interest earned!
